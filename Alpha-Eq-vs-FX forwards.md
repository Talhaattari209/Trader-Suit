An equity-versus-FX strategy trades local-currency equity index futures against FX forward longs in the same currency, with equal expected volatility. The benefit of the approach is the generation of alpha. Relative positions partly immunize against global and local shocks and enhance the predictive power of non-standard “divergence factors”, such as point-in-time information on macroeconomic developments that, in accordance with theory, drive differentials between equity and FX performance.
Using a basic set of point-in-time macro factors, we generate trading signals for 15 smaller and emerging markets with sufficient liquidity. To generate signals without hindsight bias, we use the conceptual parity and sequential machine learning approaches. All signals reveal highly significant predictive power, and simulated strategies produce high risk-adjusted returns with modest correlation to standard market benchmarks.

Why and how to trade equity versus FX positions #
This post introduces a strategy that trades equity exposure against foreign-exchange exposure in smaller or emerging markets. Equity exposure refers to a position in the main local-currency equity index future, while foreign-exchange exposure denotes a forward position in the local currency versus its natural benchmark (typically the U.S. dollar or the euro). The two positions are made comparable through volatility targeting, achieved by scaling each notional amount inversely to its recent return volatility.

These two types of positions bear communal risks, such as sensitivity to international funding conditions and local economic developments. Therefore, relative positions immunize against many global and local shocks, while being predictable by specific factors that drive a wedge between equity and FX trends. Thus, running a systematic equity-versus-FX strategy promises two benefits:

First, we generate returns with reduced correlation to traditional benchmarks.
Second, we can focus on “divergence factors”, i.e., predictors of differential trends between local equity and FX exposure, for which there is ample sound economic theory. This improves the likelihood of passing empirical tests and delivering value out-of-sample.
We can trade equity versus FX positions with good liquidity in 15 “smaller” currency areas, i.e., countries excluding the U.S., the euro area, and Japan. The currencies are in alphabetical order: Australian dollar (AUD), Brazilian real (BRL), Canadian dollar (CAD), Swiss franc (CHF), British pound (GBP), Indian rupee (INR), Korean won (KRW), Mexican peso (MXN), Malaysian ringgit (MYR), Polish zloty (PLN), Swedish krona (SEK), Thai baht (THB), Turkish lira (TRY), Taiwanese dollar (TWD), and South African rand (ZAR). The base currency for most FX positions in the U.S. dollar, except for CHF, PLN, and SEK, for which it is the euro, and GBP and TRY for which it is an equally weighted basket of EUR and USD.

Generic target returns are taken from the J.P. Morgan Macrosynergy Quantamental System (JPMaQS).

The equity leg is represented by the excess return of the front future of the main country equity index in % of risk capital on position scaled to 10% (annualized) volatility target (documentation).
The FX leg is represented by the 1-month FX forward return, expressed as a % of risk capital, scaled to a 10% (annualized) volatility target, assuming a roll back to full 1-month maturity at the end of the month (documentation).

Equity returns and FX volatility-targeted returns have been mostly positively correlated across currency areas. However, most cross-correlations have been small. This suggests that we receive considerable diversification benefits from trading this type of strategy across multiple countries as opposed to a single global basket.



Macro factor candidates #
Testable macro factors for a leveraged equity-versus-FX strategy must be grounded in macro-quantamental indicators, that is, point-in-time information states of economically meaningful metrics. Meaningful implies that the information enhances understanding of the underlying economy and corresponds to metrics commonly monitored by economists and market publications. Point-in-time means that, at each timestamp, the indicator reflects the most up-to-date value that market participants could have observed at that moment. Macro-quantamental indicators are available from the J.P. Morgan Macrosynergy Quantamental System.

A macro-quantamental factor is a composite of multiple macro-quantamental indicators, grouped to represent a single conceptual theme, for example, inflationary pressure. The purpose of such a conceptual factor is to predict returns on financial contracts. In this analysis, we constructed a small and basic set of factors that, according to standard economic theory, should positively influence the differential between local equity and FX forward returns. Broadly, these factors capture drivers of local monetary easing, increases in local-currency liquidity, and differences in risk premia between equity and FX exposures. Positive influences translate into positive predictive signals under the assumption of rational inattention (view post), whereby market participants deliberately ignore certain information when its perceived costs exceed its immediate benefits.

We calculate both absolute and relative versions of most conceptual macro factors. Relative here means we subtract the benchmark currency area’s values from the local values. This duality reflects that both versions are relevant, with absolute developments typically dominating equity returns and relative developments driving FX returns. Thus, the macro-quantamental factors below, except for the last one, are computed in three steps:

We calculate absolute and relative versions of each quantamental indicator versus the dominant currency benchmark.
Both absolute and relative indicators are sequentially normalized around their hypothetical neutral level (typically zero) using the full data panel up to each point in time and capping values at three standard deviations; this yields indicator scores.
The absolute and relative scores are separately averaged and sequentially re-normalized.
The six basic conceptual macro-quantamental factors and their constituent indicators are as follows:

Inflation shortfall is the difference between an inflation target and actual inflation, as well as its dynamics. Sub-par inflation is indicative of economic slack and supports easy monetary policy, both of which are conducive to currency weakness. As the target, we use the effective inflation target of JPMaQS (documentation), which considers both the official target value and past compliance. Actual inflation is represented by the annual and headline core CPI inflation (documentation here and here). Wherever possible, annual inflation metrics are complemented with their associated early estimates in both (documentation here and here). We also include point-in-time metrics of 3-month decreases in annual headline and core inflation (documentation here and here). All excess inflation rates and changes are scaled by dividing by the inflation target (but with a minimum of 2%) to make excesses and changes comparable across high- and low-inflation countries and periods.
Labor market weakness measures the slack in the labor market. If the economy is far from full employment, wage growth becomes typically soft, and monetary policy turns more accommodative, benefiting equity and harming FX positions. The constituent quantamental indicators are the annual change in unemployment rate (documentation) and the latest unemployment rate vs the trailing five-year average (documentation).
Demand shortfall is the negative difference between consumer spending growth and reasonable long-term benchmarks. Sluggish spending calls for policy support and tolerance for currency weakness. The quantamental indicators used for its calculation rely on both real and nominal dynamics. The former include annual real consumption growth (documentation) and annual real retail sales growth (documentation) relative to a 5-year mean of real GDP growth (documentation), while the latter is represented by annual nominal retail sales growth (documentation) relative to a 5-year mean of nominal GDP growth incorporating the effective inflation target.
Liquidity growth measures recent trends in estimated central bank (base) money injections in the economy associated with FX interventions or security purchases as a share of GDP. Past central bank interventions are good predictors of future interventions, and liquidity growth generally favors equity market performance. The constituent quantamental indicators are 3-month and 6-month changes in intervention liquidity as a ratio to GDP (documentation).
Real money and credit growth refers to the difference between inflation-adjusted broad money and private credit expansion and medium-term GDP growth. Generous credit and money supply support corporate funding and capital outflows, thus favoring equity over FX performance. We rely on three quantamental categories capturing the credit expansion beyond nominal trends: annual real broad money (documentation) and private credit growth (documentation) in excess of a 5-year mean of real GDP growth, and annual private credit expansion as a ratio to GDP (documentation).
Carry differentials and real interest rates inform about relative risk premia and implicit subsidies paid on equity versus FX risk. High real equity carry relative to FX carry suggests that premia are larger for the equity leg. All other things equal, real local-currency interest rates reduce equity carry and increase FX carry. Real here means adjusted for expected inflation. The underlying quantamental indicators are vol-targeted real equity index carry (documentation) in excess of vol-targeted real FX carry measured for Forward contracts with maturity of 1 month, 6 months, and 1 year (documentation hereand here), as well as real interest rates for 1-year (documentation) and 2-year swap contracts (documentation) according to Macrosynergy methodology.
Unlike the other factors, this one does not need to consider relative values.
The six base factor types yield 11 factor scores, as five of them have both absolute and relative versions. For all 11 conceptual factor scores, the theoretically expected predictive direction for equity-versus-FX returns is positive. This consistency enables the imposition of theoretical directional priors in machine-learning models.

Signal generation without hindsight bias #
Hindsight bias in trading-strategy development inflates the apparent predictive power and backtested performance of signals by incorporating information that would not have been available at the time a simulated position was taken. This bias can arise through casual or deliberate data mining, as well as from the influence of remembered past events on our theoretical reasoning and factor selection. There are two principal methods for mitigating such bias when combining factors into signals:

Conceptual parity simply averages the scores of all plausible and conceptually distinct factors.
Sequential machine learning relies solely on empirical analysis to select and weigh factors, but does so “point-in-time”; for each signal date, statistical learning is based only on concurrently available data samples.
The two approaches have different strengths and weaknesses.

Conceptual parity best diversifies value-generating principles. Hence, when applied with integrity and sound theory (as opposed to data mining), it can be quite robust in an unknown future. Moreover, stable linear combinations of factors avoid signal noise that arises from variations in models and hyperparameters in machine learning processes.
By contrast, sequential machine learning reduces model bias that comes from flawed theoretical priors and data quality issues by following empirical guidance. It is also more adaptable to structural changes.
In general, conceptual parity is preferable when the strategy developer has strong theoretical priors but only a limited history of relevant data. Sequential machine learning may be more effective when theoretical guidance is weak and ample data and history are available.

In this analysis, we apply both approaches to the same datasets. Doing so requires some degree of imputation of missing data, i.e., replacing unavailable macro-quantamental indicator values with estimates. Imputation is beneficial because regression-based machine learning can only operate on periods and cross-sections with a complete factor set; if even a single factor is missing, all information from the remaining factors for that observation would be discarded. To preserve this valuable information, we replace missing country-level factor values with global averages, provided that at least 40% of countries report the corresponding indicator on that date.

We calculate conceptual parity scores by averaging across all available factor scores within each country, renormalizing these averages using point-in-time signal panels at a monthly frequency, and capping absolute values at three standard deviations. Timelines of the conceptual parity scores are plotted in the facet below. Beyond short-term volatility, these have shown most of their variation in longer cycles. The monthly autocorrelation of signal values has been high, between roughly 70% for Taiwan and over 90% in Brazil. The equity bias of the signal has averaged approximately 65% since 2000.


Cross-country correlations of equity-versus-FX conceptual parity signals have been predominantly positive but generally modest. While some regional clusters exhibit higher correlations, above 50%, most pairs display much lower levels. This pattern aligns with the cross-country correlation structure of returns and underscores that equity-versus-FX positions are not merely expressions of a common global trade, but instead incorporate substantial country-specific information.


Signal generation with sequential machine learning requires three essential steps, which are a condensation of the broader method described in previous posts (e.g., “Macro trading signal optimization: basic statistical learning methods“). Here, these steps are applied to monthly-frequency data panels of factor scores and target returns:

First, we select candidates of models and hyperparameters for predicting target equity-versus-FX vol-targeted returns. To limit the computation time of the accompanying code, we are content with using two small hyperparameter grids separately for two basic model candidates. The first is regularized non-negative least squares regression, a Ridge model that only allows positive coefficient values (i.e., enforces directional theoretical priors) and penalizes the square of the coefficient estimates. This type of model is suitable for predictions with limited, correlated datasets that require theoretical priors to improve the bias-variance trade-off in machine learning. Hyperparameters include regularization strength and regression intercept. The second base model is random forest regression with monotonicity constraints. This model also enforces the positive direction of the conceptual factor impact, but allows non-linear relations. Hyperparameters include the size of the forest and various tree construction rules.
Next, we determine the rules for cross-validation, i.e., the evaluation of model versions through multiple separate training and test sets within the sequentially expanding development data set. For once, we must set the splitters that divide our panel data into valid training and validation sets in multiple folds. Here, we use an expanding K-fold panel split (ExpandingKFoldPanelSplit) that respects both the panel structure of the dataset and the chronology of observations within the development dataset. Specifically, we opt for starting with three folds and increasing to five folds alongside the increasing length of the datasets, so that the learning process can generalize better as more data points become available while maintaining a sufficiently large test set. The evaluation criterion used here is a naïve Sharpe ratio (sharpe_ratio) derived from a simple strategy where the trader goes long by a single unit when the predictions are positive and short by a single unit when the predictions are negative.
Based on model candidates and rules, we perform the actual sequential optimization with the Macrosynergy package’s specialized Python class (SignalOptimizer). It applies scikit-learn cross-validation to the specific format of macro-quantamental panel data progressively at a monthly frequency. Specifically, here we used panel data from 2000 to start selecting optimal models and calculating such optimized signals from 2002. Periods where FX markets have not been tradable are excluded from signal generation.
The model version heatmap below shows that the regression-based learning process failed to clearly converge on a single model version through 2025, but recently preferred the inclusion of an intercept to modify the long bias of signals and a rather modest regularization strength.

The bar chart below shows the average annual coefficient size for the absolute and relative conceptual factor scores. Predictive power and value generation #
The first and most important finding is that all signal variants exhibited positive and statistically significant predictive power for volatility-targeted equity-versus-FX returns across the panel. In other words, signal variation, both over time and across currencies, has been positively associated with subsequent target returns, with the probability of these relationships arising by chance being effectively zero. Forward correlation coefficients were highest for the conceptual parity signal and lowest for the random forest–based signal.


Monthly accuracy (ratio of correctly predicted relative return directions) and balanced accuracy (average ratio of correctly predicted positive and negative returns) have both been well above 50% for all signals. Indeed, all accuracy ratios ranged between 53% and 55%, which is high by the standards of macro strategies. By these metrics, machine learning signals have performed better than conceptual parity.



We simulate the economic value of the generated signals using standard naïve trading rules from Macrosynergy research articles. Month-end normalized signals of the three types determine positions for the start of the next month, allowing for one-day slippage in trading. Signal size is capped at three standard deviations as basic risk control. The PnLs ignore transaction costs and compounding. Positions are scaled to a 10% annualized long-term PnL volatility, merely for easier visual comparison of risk-adjusted returns.

The chart below displays long-term naïve PnLs for managed portfolios driven by the three signal types alongside a constant long equity-versus-FX volatility-targeted portfolio. All signal variants generated substantial risk-adjusted returns, significantly outperforming constant long equity exposure. The machine-learning strategies achieved Sharpe ratios of around 1.1 and Sortino ratios of 1.5–1.6, with S&P 500 correlations slightly above 20%. The conceptual parity strategy posted a Sharpe ratio of 1.2 and a Sortino ratio of 1.8, with an S&P 500 correlation of roughly 15%.

For comparison, the constant long equity portfolio delivered a Sharpe ratio of 0.6 and a Sortino ratio of 0.8, with a U.S. equity correlation close to 40%. PnL seasonality was modest for the macro strategies: the top 10% of months contributed less than 50% of total long-term PnL. In contrast, for the long-equity strategy, the top 10% of months accounted for nearly 80% of cumulative value creation.
